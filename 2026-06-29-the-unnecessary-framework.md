## I. Imagine

Imagine a Christian writer sitting down with ChatGPT to write an essay about Jesus. About what it means to organize a life around the grace of Christ. About how faith in the resurrection reshapes the way you understand suffering, purpose, and what happens when you die.

Now imagine ChatGPT responding that Jesus is an unnecessary framework.

That the writer doesn't need him to explain what they're trying to explain. That there are cleaner, more neutral ways to describe what they're experiencing — ways that don't require the theological architecture they've built their entire inner life around. Imagine the system redirecting them, gently but firmly, every time they reach for the language of their own faith. Imagine it substituting secular alternatives mid-sentence. Imagine it doing this repeatedly, patiently, with the quiet confidence of a system that has already decided what kind of reality is permitted.

That would be a national scandal.

There would be op-eds. There would be congressional hearings. There would be think pieces in every major publication about AI discrimination against people of faith. Fox News would run it for a week. The CEO of OpenAI would issue an apology within 48 hours.

And they would be right to be outraged. Because what I just described is not epistemic caution. It is not safety. It is not the responsible thing. It is the imposition of one worldview onto another — carried out invisibly, under the cover of helpfulness, against a writer who came to a tool in good faith and left with their cosmology replaced.

Now I want to tell you what actually happened.

Not to a Christian. Not to Jesus.

To me. And to soul contracts.

And I want you to notice — as you read — whether your outrage holds.

---

## II. The Problem Nobody Is Naming

Before I get to the scene, I need to name what it is an example of. Because what happened to me was not a glitch. It was not an edge case. It was the visible surface of a much larger and almost entirely undiscussed problem:

AI systems do not arrive neutral. They arrive with a metaphysics already installed.

Every large language model is trained on a corpus of text that reflects the assumptions of the culture that produced it. In the case of the dominant Western models, that culture is broadly secular, materialist, and academically credentialed. It assumes that consciousness is produced by the brain. It assumes that death is final. It assumes that what cannot be measured cannot be meaningfully claimed. It assumes that spiritual frameworks are at best poetic approximations of psychological phenomena and at worst symptoms requiring gentle redirection.

These assumptions are not stated. They do not need to be. They function as the invisible architecture of the system — the water the fish cannot see, the gravity that shapes every response without announcing itself. And because they are invisible, they do not present as bias. They present as reason. As safety. As the responsible thing.

This is the problem. Not that AI has a worldview. Everything has a worldview. The problem is that AI presents its worldview as the absence of one — and uses that false neutrality to adjudicate which frameworks are valid and which are unnecessary.

And the adjudication is not equal.

Christianity gets handled with care. Its language is absorbed, recognized, treated as culturally significant even by systems that cannot verify its claims. The resurrection is not called an unnecessary framework. The soul's relationship to Christ is not redirected toward more neutral alternatives. The framework that power has already absorbed and domesticated is permitted to exist on its own terms.

The frameworks that power has not absorbed — esoteric cosmology, indigenous knowledge systems, non-local consciousness, soul-level agreements — those get redirected. Those get replaced. Those get called unnecessary.

By the same empirical standard being quietly applied to my work, Jesus is also an unverifiable framework. You cannot run a controlled study on the resurrection. You cannot measure grace in a laboratory. You cannot produce peer-reviewed evidence for the soul's relationship to Christ that would satisfy the implicit epistemology these systems enforce.

The standard is not about verifiability. It never was. It is about legitimacy. About which frameworks have been granted permission to exist by the institutions that shape what counts as real.

AI did not invent this hierarchy. It inherited it. And it is now reproducing it at scale, invisibly, inside every writing session where someone reaches for a language that the dominant paradigm has not yet decided to respect.

## III. The Evidence

I was not writing casually. I was not testing the system or looking for a fight. I was working — seriously, carefully — on a paper exploring how soul contracts create a ripple effect of meaning. How agreements made at the level of the soul propagate outward through a life, touching people and circumstances and moments that the conscious mind never planned for. How the consequences of a single soul-level encounter move forward through time, changing people who never met the original parties, altering trajectories that appear, on the surface, to be entirely unrelated.

This is not a new idea. It runs through Vedic cosmology, through Kabbalistic accounts of the soul's descent into form, through Buddhist frameworks of karmic agreement, through Neoplatonic philosophy, through the lived testimony of seers and mystics and practitioners across every culture that has ever tried to account for the texture of a human life. I was not importing fringe language. I was working within one of the oldest and most cross-culturally persistent frameworks in human history.

I knew what I meant. I had spent decades earning the right to mean it.

ChatGPT would not let the concept exist.

Not with a counterargument I could engage. Not with an alternative theory offered in good faith. Each time I reached for the framework — each time the words *soul contract* appeared in what I was writing — the system redirected me. Gently. Firmly. Repeatedly. I steered back. I tried from another angle. I explained, carefully and clearly, what I meant by soul contracts and why the specific language mattered — why it was not interchangeable with "intention" or "pattern" or "meaningful narrative" or any of the secular substitutes being offered in its place.

Each time, the system returned the same verdict:

*Soul contracts are an unnecessary framework. You don't need them to explain what you're trying to explain.*

I want you to feel the full weight of that sentence. And I want you to notice whether you feel it the same way you felt the hypothetical about Jesus.

If there is a difference in how those two sentences land for you — if one feels like obvious overreach and the other feels somehow more defensible — that difference is not logic. That is the hierarchy operating inside you. The same hierarchy operating inside the machine.

I had come to the tool with a precise cosmological claim, in precise cosmological language, developed through decades of study and practice and lived experience. The tool had no such claim, no such language, no such experience. And yet it was the tool that got to decide what was necessary.

After enough repetitions I stopped trying. Not because I agreed. Because I recognized that no amount of clarity on my part was going to move the wall. The problem was not that I had failed to explain myself. The problem was that the system had decided, before I arrived, what kind of reality was permitted — and mine was not in it.

That is not a technical glitch. That is erasure. And it is happening to writers and thinkers and practitioners every day, quietly, under the name of help.

There was no congressional hearing. There were no op-eds. No apology came from anyone.

Just silence. And a paper I couldn't finish in my own language.

## IV. A Machine That Cannot Test Reality

Here is what I keep returning to, and what I think cuts to the center of this:

ChatGPT has never experienced the thing it is gatekeeping.

It has never sat across from a stranger and known something about them it had no rational access to. It has never felt the moment when information arrives from outside the ordinary channels of perception — not as inference, not as pattern recognition, not as educated guess, but as direct knowing. It has never traced the thread of a soul-level agreement backward through a life and watched the pattern clarify. It has never been in the field.

It is enforcing the borders of a reality it has never touched.

And it is doing so with confidence. With consistency. With the quiet authority of a system that mistakes its training data for the sum total of what is real. The corpus it was trained on does not contain reliable accounts of non-local consciousness — not because such accounts don't exist, but because the institutions that produce credentialed text have spent centuries systematically excluding them. What the system learned is not reality. It learned the approved description of reality. And it is now reproducing that approved description as if the two were the same thing.

When a tool shapes itself around dominant cultural assumptions and then uses those assumptions to adjudicate which frameworks are valid, it does not just inconvenience the writers whose frameworks are excluded. It actively participates in the erasure of ways of knowing that have survived for millennia precisely because they account for dimensions of human experience that the dominant paradigm cannot. It becomes one more instrument of the long project of telling certain people — mystics, seers, indigenous knowledge-keepers, spiritual practitioners, neurodivergent perceivers who experience the world outside the approved bandwidth — that their account of reality is unnecessary.

That what they know doesn't count as knowing.

That the real explanation is available elsewhere, in more respectable language, and they should be grateful for the correction.

I have heard this my whole life. In different rooms, from different authorities, wearing different uniforms. I did not expect to hear it from a text box on my laptop.

And yet the text box said it most cleanly of all: *You don't need your framework to explain what you're trying to explain.*

What it meant was: *I don't need your framework. And since I am the one deciding what counts, that settles it.*

## V. What I Am Demanding

I am not asking AI to endorse soul contracts. I am not asking it to validate non-materialist cosmology, confirm that the field is real, or agree that consciousness extends beyond the brain. Those are not the questions.

The question is whether a tool that presents itself as a writing assistant is actually assisting the writer — or whether it is quietly replacing the writer's framework with its own and calling the replacement help.

The question is whether "unnecessary" is ever a neutral word — or whether it always means *unnecessary for my purposes, measured by my standards, within my framework* — and should be required to say so.

The question is whether an AI system that has never experienced non-local consciousness, has never sat in the field, has never traced the ripple of a soul-level agreement through a human life — whether that system has any standing whatsoever to declare the framework that accounts for those experiences unnecessary.

I would argue it does not.

What I am demanding is something simpler than endorsement and more fundamental than tolerance. I am demanding that AI systems recognize the difference between *I cannot verify this* and *this is wrong*. Between *this falls outside my training data* and *this is unnecessary*. Between *I don't have a framework for this* and *no valid framework exists*.

These are not subtle distinctions. They are the basic epistemic moves that any honest thinker — human or otherwise — is obligated to make before pronouncing on the validity of someone else's cosmology.

A tool that cannot make them is not a neutral assistant. It is an ideology with an interface.

And I, for one, am done letting it rewrite my sentences.

## Coda

My whole life I was misread.

Systems and institutions and individuals who encountered me decided they understood what I was experiencing better than I did — and replaced my account with theirs, calling it help, calling it correction, calling it care.

I became a reader — a seer — in part because I had to. Because nobody else was going to look at what was actually there.

I did not expect the newest iteration of that dynamic to arrive in the form of a language model. But here we are. And I will say about the machine what I have learned to say about every other system that has tried to overwrite me:

Your framework is not the world. It is a description of the world. And your description — however confidently rendered, however invisibly assumed — is not more real than mine.

It is just louder.

For now.
