The Mask Spirituality Wears

Whose definition of spiritual are you still carrying?

June 10, 2026
9 min read

The Woman Behind the Mask, Célestin Nanteuil, 1830s. Lithograph. National Gallery of Art, Washington, D.C. (Gift of Frank Anderson Trapp). Public domain.

· · ·

There's an image most of us inherited without knowing it. The spiritual person owns little. They speak softly. They have transcended anger. They are not interested in money. They do not suffer. They have arrived somewhere — some elevated plateau of equanimity — and from there they look out at the rest of us with serene, compassionate distance.

We absorbed these images from books, from teachers, from the way certain traditions were filtered through a Western lens and handed to us as universal truth. Poverty as virtue. Detachment as achievement. Stillness as the evidence of depth.

The Harm in the Image

These are inherited assumptions. Unquestioned ideas about a lifestyle that most people have never actually lived, borrowed from traditions that were never built with your body, your nervous system, your history in mind.

Notice, too, who we picture when we picture the spiritual. It is almost always a man. The guru. The monk. The priest. Spiritual authority, in the Western imagination, has a face — and it is male, serene, and untouchable.

Two different cages. The same inherited architecture. The man told to erase his need. The woman told to erase her authority. Both left with a version of themselves that is quieter, smaller, and more manageable than what they actually are.

What We Do to Each Other

Someone expresses anger and we quietly decide they haven't done the work. Someone has wealth — real, abundant, chosen wealth — and we wonder what they sold out. But what we're really doing is policing a vision of spirituality we inherited and never interrogated.

This is not discernment. It is projection wearing the mask of wisdom.

What Spirituality Actually Is

The inherited image confuses spirituality with detachment. But genuine spirituality is presence — not distance. It is complete engagement with what is — with what is beautiful and luminous and worth staying for, and equally with what is painful, chaotic, and unresolved.

Consider what you are doing right now. You are reading. You are questioning. You are holding inherited ideas up to the light and turning them over, looking for the seams. That is a spiritual act.

Spirituality doesn't wear a mask. It doesn't walk the same road for everyone. It finds you where you are.

These images — the renunciate, the silent woman, the one who accepts without question — are held against me still in 2026. Against the words I speak. Against the authority I carry. Against the anger I bring to systems that have earned it. They are not relics. They are alive.

Which is exactly why I keep speaking.

Whose definition of spiritual are you still carrying?
