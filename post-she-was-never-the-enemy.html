She Was Never the Enemy

Learning to love your body after understanding what it was protecting you from.

July 8, 2026
10 min read

For most of my life, I was at war with my body.

Not in the dramatic way people sometimes describe — not with contempt or cruelty, exactly. It was quieter than that. More like a cold estrangement. My body was the thing that kept betraying me. That kept getting sick in ways no one could explain. That felt everything too much, then sometimes nothing at all. That collapsed without warning. That needed more than it was supposed to need.

I lived in my mind because my body felt like enemy territory.

Then I began to understand the architecture underneath my own life — the neurodivergence, the trauma history, the nervous system shaped by both — and the body I'd been fighting for decades finally became legible to me.

This isn't only my story. If you are autistic, ADHD, a trauma survivor, someone carrying complex PTSD, or simply someone who has never been taught how to live inside your own skin, some version of this war may be familiar to you. What follows is not a diagnosis-specific story. It's about what happens when you stop treating your body as the problem and start asking what it has been trying to tell you all along.

The Pain That Had No Name

Before I had language for any of it, I had years of experiences the medical system kept handing back to me, unsolved. Chronic pain. Fatigue so deep it felt geological — not tiredness, but a kind of gravitational pull toward stillness that no amount of sleep touched. Sensory experiences that overwhelmed me in ways I couldn't articulate. A nervous system that seemed calibrated for a world I wasn't living in.

I had MCAS — Mast Cell Activation Syndrome — long before I had a name for it. I had POTS. I had dysautonomia. I had spent years being told, in various ways, that these things were either not real, not serious, or not connected to each other.

They were connected. Research increasingly shows this cluster — joint hypermobility, dysautonomia, mast cell activation, chronic pain — appearing at elevated rates in both autistic and ADHD populations, not as separate coincidences but as a recognizable, if still emerging, pattern in neurodivergent nervous systems.

But the deeper thread — the one that took me longer to see — is that this isn't only a neurodivergence story. It's a nervous system story. And nervous systems are shaped by more than wiring. They're shaped by what happened to us.

What the Body Remembers

In the late 1990s, a large-scale study out of Kaiser Permanente and the CDC — now widely known as the ACE Study — found something the medical field wasn't fully prepared to reckon with: the more adverse experiences a person survived in childhood, the higher their risk, decades later, for heart disease, autoimmune conditions, chronic pain, and early death. Not just anxiety or depression. Physical illness, in the body, showing up years after the danger had passed.

Your body does not file trauma away neatly in the past. It keeps living inside the systems that were built to survive it.

A nervous system that spent years scanning for threat doesn't simply relax once the threat is gone. It keeps scanning. It keeps bracing. It keeps allocating resources toward vigilance instead of repair, digestion, rest, immune regulation — all the quiet maintenance work a body needs to do to stay well.

Autistic burnout, ADHD burnout, complex trauma, and the aftermath of adverse childhood experiences don't look identical. But they share a common architecture: a body that has been operating in survival mode for so long that survival became its baseline. None of this was your fault. None of this means your body is broken. It means your body did exactly what bodies are built to do: it kept you alive, using whatever it had.

Conscious Witnessing and the Practice of Coming Back

For years, I lived almost entirely in my mind, because my mind felt safer than my body. This is common among trauma survivors and neurodivergent people alike — a kind of exile upward, out of the body and into thought, because the body's signals were too dysregulated, too overwhelming, or too consistently dismissed to trust.

The way back is not more thinking. It's witnessing.

I call this practice Conscious Witnessing — the discipline of observing what is happening in your body, in real time, without immediately trying to fix it, narrate it, or explain it away. Not analyzing the sensation. Not spiraling into the story about the sensation. Just noticing: there is tightness here. There is heat here.

This is close to what somatic experiencing — a therapeutic approach developed by Peter Levine — is built on: the idea that trauma isn't only a story stored in memory, it's an incomplete physiological response stored in the body, and healing happens by slowly, safely allowing the nervous system to complete what it couldn't complete at the time.

You'll also hear this territory described through polyvagal theory, popularized by Stephen Porges — a framework many practitioners and survivors find genuinely clarifying, though it's worth knowing it's also facing serious scientific challenge from autonomic-nervous-system researchers. I share the language here as a useful map for nervous system states, not as settled neuroscience.

Whatever language you use for it, the practice is the same: slow down enough to actually notice what your body is doing, instead of overriding it, numbing it, or narrating from three feet above it.

The Spiritual Inheritance of Body-Shame

There is a reason so many of us never learned how to do this. It isn't only psychological. It's spiritual — inherited from frameworks most of us never chose and rarely examine.

For a very long time, across a great deal of Western spiritual tradition, the body was not treated as sacred. It was treated as the site of temptation. Something to be disciplined, subdued, mortified, transcended. Ascetic traditions framed the flesh as the enemy of the spirit. Purity culture, in its more modern form, inherited the same architecture: the body as a liability to be managed, a source of shame to be controlled rather than a home to be trusted.

We were handed a spiritual framework that taught disembodiment as virtue and embodiment as risk.

Other traditions tell a different story. Taoism's principle of Wu Wei asks you to move with your nature rather than against it. Tantric and Tibetan traditions have long treated the body as a sacred vessel, not a cage for the soul to escape but the very instrument through which awakening happens. Hermetic thought holds that the body reflects something larger — as above, so below.

The work, then, isn't only reparenting your nervous system. It's also a spiritual reclamation: taking back the idea that your body was never the source of your struggle. It was given to you for the journey — not despite it.

What Loving Your Body Actually Looks Like

Here's the problem: most people have never been taught what a good relationship with their body would even feel like. We know what estrangement feels like. We know what managing, optimizing, tolerating feels like. But love? Nobody handed us a template.

I think it starts with awe.

Right now — without your permission, without your effort, without you thinking about it even once — your heart is pumping. Millions of cells are working in wordless unison to keep you breathing, to keep you alive, to keep you here. That is not a small thing. That is magnificent.

And the pain — the flares, the collapses, the symptoms with no clean name — that pain is not the system failing you. It's the system suffering, and asking, in the only language available to it, for your attention.

So ask yourself, honestly: How do you treat your body? How do you speak to her? Do you listen at all — or do you numb the signal just to make it quiet? How often are you actually present inside your body, instead of narrating your life from somewhere just above it?

What if you cared for your body the way you care for someone you love deeply? What are you putting inside of her? Nourishment is one of the most direct paths back into the body — not a diet, not a rulebook, but real attention to what you feed the thing that is keeping you alive.

What if the body was never the obstacle? What if she was sacred all along — not a cage for the soul's journey, but the vessel given for it?

For Anyone Still in the War

If you are reading this and you are still in the estrangement — still fighting a body whose signals feel like chaos, still waiting for someone in a clinical setting to believe you — I want to say this directly: your body is not wrong. It is speaking in a language most of the world was never trained to hear.

The diagnosis, the insight, the label — none of it is the end of the story. It's the beginning of the translation.

She has been faithful to you. She deserves to be loved. Not despite everything. Because of it.
