The Shadows of Attachment Theory

Devillanizing the Avoidant — and the truth both sides need to hear.

May 6, 2026
10 min read
· · ·

I've noticed a trend in the attachment theory world online: avoidants have become the villain of the story. The one who can't love right. The one who triggers abandonment wounds on purpose, who strings people along, who just won't open up. And the anxiously attached person is cast as the one who simply "loved too much."

It's a clean narrative. It performs beautifully. But it is missing half the truth. This narrative is keeping both people stuck.

What's Actually Happening in That Dynamic

The anxious-avoidant pairing is often called the "trap" of attachment theory — and both people are wounded, both are triggered, both are reacting from survival patterns. But online, the narrative has collapsed into a hero and a villain.

What that framing completely ignores: the anxious partner's behavior is also dysregulating. Protest behaviors — excessive texting, escalating emotional demands, monitoring, pushing for reassurance repeatedly — are genuinely activating to an avoidant nervous system.

Avoidants are not withholding love as punishment. They are protecting a self that once had to be surrendered to survive closeness. The withdrawal isn't strategy. It's involuntary.

What Is Actually Happening Inside the Avoidant

You don't mean to shut down. You don't plan it, you don't choose it — it just happens. But before it does, there is a feeling that builds slowly, like pressure behind glass. It starts with the messages — not one, but a sequence, each one arriving before you've had a chance to process the last.

You feel the weight of being someone else's emotional thermostat, responsible for the climate of their inner world, accountable for every degree of their comfort or fear. And the more they reach, the smaller you become — not because they are a bad person, but because something in you remembers this feeling.

What nobody tells you is that you are grieving too. Every time you pull away from someone you actually want, there is a quiet devastation in it. You wanted to stay. You just didn't know how to do that and remain yourself at the same time.

The Wound the Anxious Partner Isn't Looking At

Relentless pursuit is not just love. Escalating need, constant reassurance-seeking, monitoring someone's response times, interpreting a missed call as abandonment — these are a wound expressing itself. They are fear in motion. And fear in motion can become controlling, even when the person experiencing it genuinely believes they are simply asking to be loved.

The anxious-avoidant dynamic is not a story of the one who loves and the one who won't. It is a story of two people, each terrified of something different, each doing the thing that makes the other person's fear worse.

My Honest Advice

To the avoidant who is exhausted from being cast as the villain: you are allowed to stop accepting that role. You are allowed to name what it feels like to be monitored, to be made responsible for another person's emotional survival.

The work for both people is the same at its core: become someone who can tolerate the discomfort of closeness — or of being needed — without your nervous system treating it as a five-alarm emergency.

You deserve a love that does not require you to be smaller to survive it.
